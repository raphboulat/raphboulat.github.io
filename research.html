Criminal Representation of Minorities in Movies
with Alvise Scarabosio

Movies can have a powerful cultural influence, potentially shaping perceptions and reinforcing or challenging stereotypes. This paper analyzes around 3000 movie scripts from 1910-2022 to examine racial representation and characterization. We document that minority representation, particularly of Black characters, has increased over time but remains below US population shares, both in screen presence and in dialogue. Linguistically, Black characters employ slang and slurs more frequently compared to other racial groups. Sentiment analysis shows that Black characters are depicted more negatively than White characters, with the gap narrowing from the 1970s through the 2000s, a pattern potentially consistent with the Blaxploitation era and subsequent shifts in Black filmmaking. Building on these findings, we plan several extensions using LLMs and NLP techniques. First, we will investigate criminal and occupational portrayals of Black characters, and examine whether Black representation behind the camera (among directors and producers) correlates with improved on-screen portrayals. Second, we will analyze movie posters to assess whether Black characters receive less proportional speaking time relative to their visual presence in promotional materials, which could suggest strategic racial presentation in marketing. Finally, we plan to match ZIP code-level political attitude data (CCES) with Google Trends metrics of movie success to study whether movies with greater diversity perform differently across regions with varying political attitudes, providing a first step toward understanding the demand for racial representation in movies.
